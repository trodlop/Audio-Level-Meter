GLOSSARY

descriptions

Sound Level Display

Average Sound Level

Calculates the mean average sound level of the recorded audio interval. It defines the current sound level, and balances out instantaneous spikes and troughs in the recorded interval.

Maximum

The maximum refers to the maximum average sound level calculated. It is not the absolute maximum sound level, rather the maximum of the calculated average levels.

Minimum

The minimum refers to the minimum average sound level calculated. It is not the absolute minimum sound level, rather the minimum of the calculated average levels.

Peak

The absolute maximum instantaneous sound pressure level detected.

LEQ

LEQ is the mean average of the calculated average sound levels over the entire recording period. It accounts for fluctuations in noise intensities by averaging them over time. It is used in when comparing to reference sound levels. For example, general conversation ~65dB, this refers to the LEQ of general conversation.

Visualiser Types

Frequency Intensity Spectrum

A frequency intensity spectrum displays the intensity (in Decibels) of the sound input at each specific frequency interval, in this case ~45Hz intervals. Note that, the intensity of the sound at each interval is measured by how far it is from the maximum achievable sound level. Since each microphone has a different maximum sound level, calibration is necessary to accurately measure sound levels. It is useful for measuring the loudness specfic frequencies.

Spectrogram

A spectrogram displays the intensity of the sound input at each frequency at each point in time. Depending on selected colour for the spectrogram, different colours represent different sound intensities. Usually the brighter and "hotter" the colour, the more intense the sound level at that frequency interval. The X axis shows the time since recording (in seconds), ie. -20 means that slice was recorded 20 seconds earlier. It is useful for visualising the sound levels over time, while still giving information about loudness of individual frequencies.

Waveform

This shows the amplitude of the sound wave at a specific point in time, essentially giving a side profile of how the sound wave "looks".

Min-Max Normalisation

This plots the intensity of each frequency interval, but uses a min-max-normalisation algorithm to normalise the intensities to between 0 and 1, and exponentiates these values resulting in vertical bands showing which frequencies are most intense. This allows for identification of particularly loud frequencies and comparison of intensities between frequencies.

Frequency Weighting

Frequency weighting refers to "weighting" certain frequencies such that they are louder or quieter. For exmaple, the human ear is more sensitive to mid-range frequencies (1 - 3kHz), and thus percieves them more loudly. We can therefore mimic how humans will percieve the sound level by boosting the levels of the mid-level frequencies.

ITU-R 468

A widely used frequency weighting, mainly used in the UK, Europe and Australia. It boosts the mid-high-range frequencies (1 - 10kHz) while reducing frequencies above and below this. It is particularly useful when measuring levels in audio systems where clarity is most important.

A

The most commonly used frequency weighting, as it most accurately mimics the human ear's frequency response. A weighting aggressively reduces the loudness of frequencies below 1kHz, while keeping the mid-high-range frequencies fairly flat.

C

C weighting is not used as often as A or ITU-R 468, however it is useful for measuring the peak sound level of very loud impulsive sources, such as heavy machinery. It has a flatter weighting than A or ITU-R 468 weightings.

Z

Z weighting or Zero weighting applies no weighting to the incoming sound level. While it is useful for measuring the actual sound level, it does not accurately mimic the frequency response of a human ear, and so actual sound levels may not reflect percieved sound levels.

Sound Level Type

Max

Simply displays the maximum sound level recorded in that instant. It is important to note that maximum sound levels do not reflect percieved sound levels. However, measuring maximum sound levels can be used to assess safe and dangerous levels.

Mean

Calculates the mean average sound level recorded in that instant. It more accurately reflects the true sound level recorded over time.

Data Smoothing

Smoothing functions are used to smooth out the recorded sound data. Different smoothing functions can be used to achieve different results, for example, to smooth out anomalous peaks and troughs, while still keeping the genaral shape of the dataset. Note that smoothing functions tend to be quite computationally intensive requiring many calculations, and can thus slow the performance of the audio level meter, especially if using an older device.

Raw (no smoothing)

This applies no smoothing to the data, keeping the data raw. Applying no smoothing to the data can help identify particularly high sound levels.

Savitzky-Golay Filter

A Savitzky-Golay Filter is a smoothing function used to smooth out anomalous data points, while still keeping the shape of the dataset. It makes it easier to see the general shape of the data, as it smoothes out the "bumpiness".

Moving Average

A moving average smoothing function functions the same as a Savitzky-Golay Filter, however replaces each value with the mean/median average of the values in a sliding window, resulting in a more aggressive smoothing of the data than a Savitzky-Golay Filter, but being less computationally intensive.

Data Download Type

This specifies what audio data you want to download from the recorded interval. The downloaded data is as a .txt file.

Simplified

This will only download the min, max, peak, etc. data, as well as the different calibration, frequency weighting and data smoothing settings that were selected.

Full

This will download all of the same data as simplified, however, also downloads the full captured audio data for that recorded interval. The downloaded audio data is in the form of a 512 long float32Array for each time interval (100ms). Each array contains the decibel levels for each frequency interval (in the form: Float32Array(512) [Float32, Float32, Float32, ...).

FAQ

faq

Is this audio level meter free?

Yes. The audio level meter is completely free to use, and does not have any additional features hidden behind a paywall. The aim of this project is to provide a high quality audio level meter completely free for people to use.

Does this audio level meter collect any personal information?

The audio level meter does not collect, use or save any personal information. The only data/information the website may use regards the system's microphone as it is needed to capture audio data for the meter.

Can I use this audio level meter professionally or for non-recreational purposes?

This audio level meter aims to be as high quality as possible, and includes any useful features which makes it very versatile. However, I do not recommend ONLY using this audio level meter to monitor audio levels. Even if you are using a prossessional microphone to capture the audio, you will still have to calibrate the meter against a control. For this reason I would recommend using this meter in conjunction with another control meter, to make sure the meter is accurate.

Does the meter require any specific hardware (any particular microphones or audio capturing hardware) to function properly?

The audio level meter will work with any microphone (including in-built phone or laptop microphone as well as external microphones), however, the quality of the microphone will affect accuracy of the displayed audio data. If you are using a lower quality microphone, you may try to edit the filter settings to counteract the microphone's frequency response.

How can I choose between microphones if I have multiple connected?

Currently there is no option to select between microphones, however this feature will get added in the near future. On computers the microphone used for the meter is the system default microphone (this can be changed in the computer's settings). If this does not work or you are using a phone, for now, just have the microphone you want to use be the only one plugged in.

How often does this audio level meter get updated?

Depending on what needs to be added or fixed, updates may vary in frequency. However, I try to maintain the website as much as possible.

How can I report any bugs I find?

There is currently no in-built way of reporting any bugs found, however, you can drop me an email with any information regarding the bug: trodlop2004@gmail.com

Will this audio level meter ever be turned into an application?

While I am not currently working on turning this meter into an app, I do have plans to make it into a Windows desktop application. For the time being, I have no plans of turning it into a phone application.

Note:

This audio level meter is open source and the source code may be reviewed on my GitHub: https://github.com/trodlop/Audio-Level-Meter. Feel free to download and edit the code however you like.
